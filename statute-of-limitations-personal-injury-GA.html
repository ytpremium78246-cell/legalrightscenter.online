Georgia Statute of Limitations

Court deadlines and filing requirements in the state of Georgia.

📅 Last Reviewed: June 2026
✍ Reviewed By: Michael E. Vance, Esq.

🍑 How Long Do You Have to Sue in Georgia?

In Georgia, the court deadlines for filing a lawsuit are strictly enforced. The statute of limitations for personal injury claims is 2 years (O.C.G.A. § 9-3-33).

If you fail to file a lawsuit in a civil court before this deadline, the court will almost certainly dismiss your case, barring you from recovering any compensation.

When Does the Clock Start ticking?

Generally, the statute of limitations clock starts ticking on the date of the accident. However, there are a few important exceptions in Georgia:

  • The Discovery Rule: If an injury was not immediately apparent (such as internal organ damage or toxic chemical exposure), the clock may start on the date you discovered, or should have discovered, the injury.
  • Minors: If the victim was a minor at the time of the injury, the statute of limitations may be paused (tolled) until they turn 18.
  • Out-of-State Defendant: If the defendant leaves the state of Georgia after the accident, the timeline might be paused for the period they are away.

Tolling Exceptions and Administrative Deadlines in Georgia

While the statute of limitations deadline is strictly applied by civil courts, Georgia law permits the tolling (pausing) of the timeline under rare, legally defined exceptions:

  • The Discovery Rule: If an injury was latent and could not have been reasonably discovered on the date of the incident (such as a surgical sponge left in a patient or a toxic chemical exposure), the clock may be tolled until the patient discovered, or should have discovered, the harm.
  • Minority of the Plaintiff: If the injured party was a minor (under the age of 18) when the accident occurred, state statutes may toll the limitations period until their 18th birthday, depending on the case type.
  • Mental Incapacity: If the plaintiff is deemed legally incompetent or mentally incapacitated at the time of the injury, the timeline is paused until the incapacity is resolved.

Government Claims Notice Deadline: If your injury involved a government entity—such as a collision with a public city transit bus, a slip on state property, or negligence at a county hospital—you cannot file a standard lawsuit immediately. You must first submit a formal administrative notice of claim. In Georgia, this administrative filing window is extremely short, typically ranging from 90 to 180 days. Failing to submit this notice within the deadline permanently waives your right to sue the government.

Section I: The Civil Litigation Process in Personal Injury Claims

Navigating the United States legal system after a severe accident or medical error can be an overwhelming experience. To help you understand the path forward, this detailed roadmap explains each milestone of the civil litigation process, from initial fact investigation through discovery, trial, and potential appellate review.

1. Fact Investigation and Pre-Filing Protocols

The foundation of any successful legal action begins long before a complaint is filed in civil court. Your legal team must perform a comprehensive investigation to gather and preserve critical evidence. This phase includes:

  • Securing Official Accident Reports: Retrieving the official police crash report, highway patrol logs, or municipal incident logs.
  • Medical Record Retrieval: Compiling complete medical charts, diagnostic radiology scans (MRIs, CT scans), billing statements, and physician notes from all treating providers.
  • Wage Verification: Documenting financial losses by obtaining employer verification letters, tax records, and pay stubs to calculate lost income and lost earning capacity.
  • Spoliation Prevention: Sending formal spoliation of evidence letters to trucking companies, property owners, or manufacturers to legally compel them to preserve event data, cell phone records, or defective product components.

2. Filing the Civil Complaint and Summons

Litigation officially commences when your attorney files a Complaint in the appropriate civil court (such as the county superior or circuit court). The complaint outlines the factual allegations of the incident, defines the legal theories of negligence or strict product liability, and specifies the financial damages sought. The court then issues a Summons, which must be formally served on the defendant by a licensed process server, giving them notice of the action.

3. The Defendant's Answer and Affirmative Defenses

Upon service of process, the defendant (typically represented by attorneys hired by their liability insurance carrier) must file a formal Answer within a strict timeframe (typically 20 to 30 days depending on the jurisdiction). The answer responds to each paragraph of the complaint—admitting, denying, or claiming lack of information. The answer also asserts affirmative defenses, such as claiming the plaintiff was partially responsible (comparative negligence) or that the filing occurred after the statute of limitations expired.

4. The Pre-Trial Discovery Phase

Discovery is the longest and most labor-intensive stage of civil litigation. It is the formal pre-trial period where both sides are legally required to exchange all relevant information and evidence. Discovery is executed through three main channels:

  • Written Interrogatories: Formal written questions that each party must answer under oath.
  • Requests for Production: Subpoenaing documents, emails, maintenance files, internal corporate communications, and physical evidence.
  • Oral Depositions: Questioning witnesses, treating physicians, and the parties themselves under oath. A court reporter transcribes the testimony, which can be used to impeach witnesses at trial.

5. Pre-Trial Motions and Summary Judgment

Attorneys file various motions to resolve legal disputes before trial. A key motion is the Motion for Summary Judgment, where the defense argues that there are no genuine disputes of material fact and that they are entitled to judgment as a matter of law. If granted, the case is dismissed before reaching a jury.

6. Alternative Dispute Resolution and Mediation

Because trial is risky, expensive, and time-consuming, judges frequently order the parties to participate in mediation. During mediation, a neutral third-party mediator reviews the case files, meets with both sides, and attempts to broker a voluntary settlement. Over 95% of personal injury and mass tort cases settle during this phase.

7. Jury Trial, Verdict, and Appellate Review

If settlement cannot be reached, the case proceeds to trial. A jury is selected, and both sides deliver opening statements, call witnesses, submit evidence, and make closing arguments. The jury then deliberates to render a verdict. The losing party has the right to file an appeal with a higher appellate court, arguing that legal errors occurred during the trial.

Section II: Rules of Evidence and Documentation Standards

Proving a civil claim requires establishing liability by a preponderance of the evidence. Having the right documentation can force insurance adjusters to settle your claim without the need for a protracted lawsuit.

1. Understanding Spoliation of Evidence

Spoliation is the intentional or negligent destruction, alteration, or concealment of evidence relevant to a legal proceeding. Under federal and state civil codes, if a defendant destroys evidence after receiving notice of a claim, the court can penalize them with an "adverse inference" instruction. This tells the jury to assume that the destroyed evidence would have been harmful to the defendant's position. To protect your rights, your attorney must send a detailed litigation hold letter immediately after the accident.

2. Electronic Logs, Telematics, and Black Box Data

In vehicle collisions, especially those involving 18-wheelers or commercial vans, electronic evidence is critical. Commercial trucks are equipped with Event Data Recorders (EDRs) that capture speed, brake application, throttle position, and engine parameters in the seconds surrounding a crash. Additionally, GPS logs and Electronic Logging Devices (ELDs) provide an indisputable record of hours-of-service compliance, helping prove driver fatigue.

3. Witness Declarations and Expert Witness Testimony

Eyewitness testimonies provide independent validation of how an accident occurred. Your legal team will take recorded statements or declarations from witnesses. In addition, complex injury claims require expert testimony under FRE Rule 702. Experts such as accident reconstructionists, biomechanical engineers, orthopedic surgeons, and life care planners compile reports detailing the mechanics of the impact, the physiological cause of the injuries, and the lifetime financial cost of rehabilitation.

Section III: Navigating Insurance Negotiations and Claims Adjusters

In most personal injury and property damage claims, your primary interactions will be with insurance adjusters. Understanding the business model of insurance companies is key to protecting your financial recovery.

1. First-Party vs. Third-Party Claims

A first-party claim is filed with your own auto, health, or property insurance provider. A third-party claim is filed against the insurance company of the negligent party who caused the accident. While your own insurance company has a contractual duty to handle your claim fairly, third-party adjusters have no fiduciary duty to you. Their sole objective is to pay you as little as possible to close the file.

2. How Insurance Companies Use Software to Score Claims

Major liability insurers use automated claims evaluation software (such as Colossus) to calculate settlement offers. The software analyzes variables like the specific ICD-10 medical diagnostic codes, the credentials of the treating physician, and the historical jury verdicts in the local county. Knowing how these databases function allows experienced attorneys to structure demand letters that emphasize the specific data points that maximize the claim score.

3. Recognizing Insurer Bad Faith and Legal Remedies

Every insurance policy is bound by an implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing. If an insurer denies coverage without performing a reasonable investigation, delays communication for weeks, misrepresents policy language, or makes low-ball offers that ignore documented bills, they are operating in bad faith. If bad faith is proven, you can file a lawsuit against the insurer to recover compensation beyond the initial policy limits, including damages for emotional distress, attorney fees, and punitive damages.
