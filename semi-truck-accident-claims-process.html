Semi-Truck Accident Claims Process

Commercial truck accident claims involve federal regulations, multiple defendants, and evidence that disappears quickly. This step-by-step guide explains the process from the accident scene to settlement or trial.

📅 Last Reviewed: June 2026

How Commercial Vehicle Accident Claims Differ from Standard Car Accident Claims

If you've been in an accident with a semi-truck, tractor-trailer, or other commercial motor vehicle, you are in fundamentally different legal territory than a typical car accident claim. Here's why:

  • Federal regulatory layer: FMCSA regulations (49 CFR) govern commercial vehicles and their drivers — violations of these regulations create actionable evidence of negligence that doesn't exist in passenger car cases
  • Multiple defendants: Driver, trucking company, cargo loader, manufacturer, and maintenance contractors may all share liability
  • Higher insurance minimums: Commercial carriers are required to carry minimum $750,000 liability coverage for property-carrying vehicles; $5,000,000 for hazmat
  • More evidence: ELD data, black box data, maintenance records, driver qualification files, drug/alcohol tests
  • Faster evidence destruction: Carriers are only required to retain certain records for 6 months — preservation demands must be sent immediately

Step 1: Immediate Actions After a Truck Accident

  • Call 911 — ensure police and medical response
  • Photograph everything at the scene: truck markings (DOT number, company name, trailer number), damage, skid marks, road conditions, your injuries
  • Get the truck driver's CDL number, employer name, and insurance information
  • Record the truck's USDOT number (on the door) — this allows lookup of the carrier's FMCSA record at safer.fmcsa.dot.gov
  • Get witness contact information
  • Seek medical attention immediately — even if you feel fine
  • Do not speak to the trucking company's insurance adjuster without legal counsel

Step 2: Investigate the Carrier's FMCSA Safety Record

The FMCSA's Safety Measurement System (SMS) scores all motor carriers on seven safety behavior analysis and safety improvement categories (BASICs). Carriers with high scores in Unsafe Driving, Hours-of-Service Compliance, or Vehicle Maintenance are known safety risks. This data is publicly available at ai.fmcsa.dot.gov/SMS and can be powerful evidence of a pattern of violations.

Step 3: Preservation of Evidence

Your attorney should send a litigation hold letter to the carrier within days of the accident. This letter demands preservation of: ELD data, black box data, dash cam footage, driver logbooks (if manual), driver qualification file, drug/alcohol test results, maintenance records for the vehicle, and the carrier's FMCSA inspection history for the vehicle.

Step 4: Establishing Liability

Liability in truck accident cases typically flows through one or more of these legal theories:

  • Respondeat superior: The employer is liable for the employee-driver's negligence committed in the scope of employment
  • Negligent entrustment: The company entrusted the vehicle to a driver they knew or should have known was unqualified or dangerous
  • Negligent maintenance: The company failed to maintain the vehicle in compliance with FMCSA standards
  • Direct negligence: The company's own policies violated safety standards (e.g., pressuring drivers to violate HOS rules)

Step 5: Insurance and Damages

Commercial carriers must carry substantially higher insurance than passenger vehicles. Minimum federal coverage requirements for property-carrying CMVs: $750,000 for most freight; $5,000,000 for hazardous materials. State requirements may be higher. Total available insurance coverage is often a key factor in case value — your attorney will identify all insurance policies (primary, excess/umbrella) covering the carrier and vehicle.

⚑ FLAG FOR HUMAN REVIEW: Insurance minimum figures reflect current FMCSA requirements under 49 CFR § 387.9. Verify these amounts have not changed, particularly for specialized freight categories. Source: ecfr.gov.

Adhering to Federal Motor Carrier Safety Standards

In addition to local state highway traffic regulations, all interstate commercial carriers must conform to strict rules promulgated by the Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration (FMCSA). Under 49 CFR Part 390, a commercial vehicle is defined by its weight (over 10,001 pounds) or cargo type. If a carrier allows an unqualified driver to operate, or fails to maintain detailed maintenance and vehicle inspection files under 49 CFR Part 396, it has committed a direct violation. In court, proving that a carrier violated these regulations can establish negligence per se, meaning the carrier is automatically deemed to have breached its duty of care to the public.

Furthermore, commercial policies typically contain an MCS-90 endorsement. Required by the Motor Carrier Act of 1980, this endorsement is a public-safety guarantee. It mandates that the insurer must pay any final judgment rendered against the carrier for public liability resulting from negligence in interstate commerce, up to the statutory minimum, even if the policy itself has exclusions or the carrier breached its contractual terms.

Frequently Asked Questions: Commercial Vehicle Liability

The MCS-90 endorsement ensures that if a commercial truck causes an accident, the insurance company cannot refuse to pay an injured third party due to the carrier's policy breaches, such as failing to report the accident, driving unlicensed, or carrying excluded cargo. The insurer pays the victim and can later seek reimbursement from the trucking company.
Under FMCSA regulations, if a motor carrier leases a truck and driver (an owner-operator), the driver is treated as a "statutory employee" of the carrier. This prevents trucking companies from avoiding vicarious liability by claiming the driver was an independent contractor rather than an employee.
Electronic Logging Devices (ELDs) automatically record a vehicle's motion and engine status, linking it to the driver's duty status. Attorneys cross-reference ELD logs with GPS records, toll receipts, and fuel stop transaction times to identify log falsification or Hours of Service (HOS) violations.

Section I: The Civil Litigation Process in Personal Injury Claims

Navigating the United States legal system after a severe accident or medical error can be an overwhelming experience. To help you understand the path forward, this detailed roadmap explains each milestone of the civil litigation process, from initial fact investigation through discovery, trial, and potential appellate review.

1. Fact Investigation and Pre-Filing Protocols

The foundation of any successful legal action begins long before a complaint is filed in civil court. Your legal team must perform a comprehensive investigation to gather and preserve critical evidence. This phase includes:

  • Securing Official Accident Reports: Retrieving the official police crash report, highway patrol logs, or municipal incident logs.
  • Medical Record Retrieval: Compiling complete medical charts, diagnostic radiology scans (MRIs, CT scans), billing statements, and physician notes from all treating providers.
  • Wage Verification: Documenting financial losses by obtaining employer verification letters, tax records, and pay stubs to calculate lost income and lost earning capacity.
  • Spoliation Prevention: Sending formal spoliation of evidence letters to trucking companies, property owners, or manufacturers to legally compel them to preserve event data, cell phone records, or defective product components.

2. Filing the Civil Complaint and Summons

Litigation officially commences when your attorney files a Complaint in the appropriate civil court (such as the county superior or circuit court). The complaint outlines the factual allegations of the incident, defines the legal theories of negligence or strict product liability, and specifies the financial damages sought. The court then issues a Summons, which must be formally served on the defendant by a licensed process server, giving them notice of the action.

3. The Defendant's Answer and Affirmative Defenses

Upon service of process, the defendant (typically represented by attorneys hired by their liability insurance carrier) must file a formal Answer within a strict timeframe (typically 20 to 30 days depending on the jurisdiction). The answer responds to each paragraph of the complaint—admitting, denying, or claiming lack of information. The answer also asserts affirmative defenses, such as claiming the plaintiff was partially responsible (comparative negligence) or that the filing occurred after the statute of limitations expired.

4. The Pre-Trial Discovery Phase

Discovery is the longest and most labor-intensive stage of civil litigation. It is the formal pre-trial period where both sides are legally required to exchange all relevant information and evidence. Discovery is executed through three main channels:

  • Written Interrogatories: Formal written questions that each party must answer under oath.
  • Requests for Production: Subpoenaing documents, emails, maintenance files, internal corporate communications, and physical evidence.
  • Oral Depositions: Questioning witnesses, treating physicians, and the parties themselves under oath. A court reporter transcribes the testimony, which can be used to impeach witnesses at trial.

5. Pre-Trial Motions and Summary Judgment

Attorneys file various motions to resolve legal disputes before trial. A key motion is the Motion for Summary Judgment, where the defense argues that there are no genuine disputes of material fact and that they are entitled to judgment as a matter of law. If granted, the case is dismissed before reaching a jury.

6. Alternative Dispute Resolution and Mediation

Because trial is risky, expensive, and time-consuming, judges frequently order the parties to participate in mediation. During mediation, a neutral third-party mediator reviews the case files, meets with both sides, and attempts to broker a voluntary settlement. Over 95% of personal injury and mass tort cases settle during this phase.

7. Jury Trial, Verdict, and Appellate Review

If settlement cannot be reached, the case proceeds to trial. A jury is selected, and both sides deliver opening statements, call witnesses, submit evidence, and make closing arguments. The jury then deliberates to render a verdict. The losing party has the right to file an appeal with a higher appellate court, arguing that legal errors occurred during the trial.
