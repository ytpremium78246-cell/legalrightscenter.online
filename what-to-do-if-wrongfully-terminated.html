What Should I Do If I Am Wrongfully Terminated From My Job?

Wrongful termination is more than losing a job — it is a violation of your legal rights. This comprehensive guide walks you through every critical step, from the moment of termination through potential litigation and recovery of damages.

📅 Last Reviewed: June 2026
✍ Reviewed By: Michael E. Vance, Esq.
Information Disclaimer: LegalRightsCenter.online is an independent information directory. The details on this page do not constitute legal advice, are not a substitute for a licensed attorney's advice, and do not create an attorney-client relationship. State laws vary and deadlines (statutes of limitations) are strict.

Defining Wrongful Termination Under U.S. Law

Wrongful termination — also called wrongful discharge — is a legal term of art that describes a firing that violates a specific law, contract, or recognized public policy. It is essential to understand that "wrongful" does not mean "unfair," "harsh," or "undeserved" in the colloquial sense. An employer can fire you for reasons you consider profoundly unjust — playing favorites, having a personality conflict with your supervisor, or simply wanting to hire someone else — and those firings, while deeply frustrating, may be entirely lawful under the at-will employment doctrine. Wrongful termination has a specific legal meaning.

A termination is legally "wrongful" when it violates one of the following:

  • Federal or state anti-discrimination statutes (e.g., you were fired because of your race, sex, religion, national origin, age, or disability).
  • Federal or state anti-retaliation statutes (e.g., you were fired for filing a workers' compensation claim, reporting OSHA violations, filing an EEOC complaint, or blowing the whistle on illegal activity).
  • An express or implied employment contract (e.g., your employee handbook promised certain termination procedures were breached).
  • Public policy (e.g., you were fired for serving on jury duty or for refusing to commit a crime at your employer's direction).
  • The Family and Medical Leave Act (FMLA) (e.g., you were fired for taking legally protected leave for a serious health condition).

Immediate Steps to Take in the First 48 Hours

The actions you take immediately after a wrongful termination can make or break your legal case. Evidence disappears, witnesses' memories fade, and legal deadlines tick from the moment of termination. Here is what to do, in order of priority:

Step 1: Control Your Reaction and Do Not Sign Anything Immediately

Employers frequently present terminated employees with separation agreements or severance packages at the moment of termination, asking for an immediate signature. These agreements almost always contain a release of all legal claims — meaning that by signing, you waive your right to sue the employer for wrongful termination, discrimination, or any other claim, in exchange for the offered severance. Under the Older Workers Benefit Protection Act (OWBPA), employees 40 and over must be given at least 21 days to consider a separation agreement (45 days in group layoffs) and 7 days to revoke after signing. Younger employees have no statutory right to deliberation time, but you should still politely decline to sign on the spot and take the document home to have an attorney review it.

Step 2: Create a Written Record of the Termination

Within hours of your termination, sit down and write a detailed, factual account of everything that occurred. Include the date, time, and location; the names and titles of everyone present; the exact words used to notify you of the termination; any reason given; and any other relevant context. Be as specific as possible. If your supervisor said, "We're letting you go because of your performance," write that down verbatim. If the HR manager said, "We can't have someone your age in this role anymore," write that down — those words could be the most valuable evidence in your case.

Step 3: Gather and Preserve All Documentation

Before you lose access to company systems, collect every document that is relevant to your employment and termination that you are legally entitled to have. This includes:

  • Your offer letter and any signed employment contracts or addenda.
  • The employee handbook or policy manual (especially the progressive discipline and termination sections).
  • All performance reviews — even those that have been favorable.
  • Any commendation letters, emails praising your work, or awards you received.
  • Any written warnings you received (and document any inconsistencies between the warning content and your actual conduct).
  • Emails, texts, or other communications that reveal discriminatory comments, shifting explanations for your termination, or differential treatment of employees outside your protected class.
  • Your pay stubs, W-2s, and benefits documentation to establish a baseline for calculating back pay.

Step 4: Request Your Personnel File

Many states — including California, Illinois, Michigan, New York, and Pennsylvania — give employees a statutory right to inspect and copy their own personnel files. Submit a written request to HR immediately. Your personnel file should contain all performance reviews, disciplinary records, and other documentation the employer maintained about you. If the file contains entries that were created after your termination or that you were never shown while employed, that can be evidence of fabricated justification — a phenomenon employment lawyers call "papering a file."

Step 5: Identify and Contact Witnesses

Think carefully about who at the company has knowledge that is relevant to your termination. This may include colleagues who witnessed discriminatory comments by management, coworkers who were treated differently than you despite similar circumstances, supervisors who praised your work shortly before you were fired, or anyone who was present during conversations about your termination. Contact these individuals promptly, while the events are fresh in their minds, and politely ask if they would be willing to speak to an attorney. Even if they are unwilling to come forward, knowing who they are and what they know is valuable intelligence for your legal team.

Step 6: File for Unemployment Insurance Immediately

Regardless of whether you intend to pursue a wrongful termination lawsuit, file for unemployment insurance benefits right away. Most states allow online applications. The benefits are available to workers who lost their jobs through no fault of their own. Your employer may contest your unemployment claim, asserting that you were fired for misconduct. The state unemployment agency will investigate, and both sides can present evidence. The adjudication of your unemployment claim can provide valuable insight into the employer's stated justification and any inconsistencies. Note that receiving unemployment benefits does not preclude you from also filing a wrongful termination lawsuit — they are separate remedies.

Understanding Your Legal Remedies and Potential Recovery

If you pursue a wrongful termination claim, the damages available to you depend on the legal theory underlying your claim and the severity of the employer's conduct. Here is a breakdown of the main categories of damages:

1. Back Pay

Back pay represents the wages, salary, bonuses, commissions, and benefits (including health insurance premium equivalents, retirement contributions, and unvested stock options) you would have earned from the date of your termination through the date of the court's judgment or settlement. Back pay is the most foundational remedy in wrongful termination cases and is available under virtually every employment discrimination and retaliation statute. Courts typically reduce back pay awards by any amounts the plaintiff earned (or could have earned with reasonable effort) from substitute employment during the back pay period — this is the "mitigation of damages" doctrine.

2. Front Pay

Front pay is intended to compensate for future earnings you will lose as a result of the wrongful termination. It is awarded when reinstatement to your former position is not feasible — for example, if returning to work alongside the supervisors who discriminated against you would be intolerable, if your former position has been permanently eliminated, or if the employer-employee relationship has been irreparably damaged. Front pay calculations typically involve comparing your projected earnings in your former position against your projected earnings in available alternative employment, discounted to present value.

3. Compensatory Damages for Emotional Distress

Federal statutes like Title VII and the ADA allow recovery for "compensatory damages" including emotional pain, suffering, inconvenience, mental anguish, and loss of enjoyment of life. The emotional toll of wrongful termination can be severe — depression, anxiety, damage to personal relationships, and loss of professional identity are common consequences. Compensatory damages require proof, which is typically provided through your own testimony, testimony from family members or therapists who observed your distress, and sometimes expert psychiatric or psychological testimony. Under Title VII and the ADA, combined compensatory and punitive damages are capped based on employer size, ranging from $50,000 (employers with 15-100 employees) to $300,000 (employers with over 500 employees). The ADEA and Equal Pay Act do not permit compensatory damages for pain and suffering — they allow liquidated damages instead.

4. Punitive Damages

Punitive damages are designed to punish employers for particularly egregious, malicious, or reckless conduct and to deter similar behavior in the future. Under Title VII and the ADA, punitive damages are available if the plaintiff can show the employer engaged in intentional discrimination with "malice or reckless indifference" to the employee's federally protected rights. The Supreme Court established this standard in Kolstad v. American Dental Association (1999). Punitive damages are subject to the same statutory caps mentioned above. State law claims, such as intentional infliction of emotional distress or state statutory claims, may allow uncapped punitive damages in some jurisdictions, making them strategically important in severe cases.

5. Attorney's Fees and Costs

A significant benefit of federal employment discrimination statutes is their fee-shifting provision: if you prevail, the court may award you reasonable attorney's fees and litigation costs to be paid by the employer. This provision allows employees who could never afford to pay an attorney's standard hourly rate to nonetheless obtain quality legal representation. Many employment attorneys take discrimination cases on contingency, advancing all costs and receiving a percentage of the final recovery only if they win — but the statutory fee-shifting provision provides an additional mechanism for attorney compensation that benefits the client by reducing the contingency percentage in some arrangements.

Section I: The EEOC Charge Filing Process — A Critical Prerequisite

Before you can file a federal lawsuit under Title VII, the ADA, ADEA, the Equal Pay Act, or GINA, you must first file a charge of discrimination with the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) or a state fair employment practices agency (FEPA). This administrative prerequisite is jurisdictional — failing to comply with it generally bars you from the federal courthouse entirely, regardless of the merits of your case.

Charge Filing Deadlines: The Most Important Dates in Employment Law

The deadline for filing an EEOC charge is either 180 calendar days or 300 calendar days from the date of the discriminatory act (in this case, the date of termination), depending on your state:

  • 300-day deadline (Deferral States): Most states have their own fair employment agency (e.g., California's DFEH, New York's DHR, Illinois's IDHR, Texas's TWC-CRD). If your state has such an agency, the EEOC charge filing deadline extends to 300 days. This covers the majority of workers in the United States.
  • 180-day deadline (Non-Deferral States): In states without their own fair employment agency, the EEOC filing deadline is only 180 days. This shorter deadline has caught many employees off guard.

These deadlines are strictly enforced. The Supreme Court has repeatedly held that EEOC charge filing deadlines are similar to statutes of limitations and can bar untimely claims. Missing the deadline by even one day can permanently forfeit your federal discrimination claim. Act immediately upon termination.

What Happens After You File an EEOC Charge?

After you file, the EEOC will notify your employer and may attempt to mediate the dispute through its voluntary mediation program — a faster, confidential process that can resolve claims without the need for a formal investigation. If mediation fails or is declined, the EEOC investigates the charge. The investigation may involve document requests to the employer, witness interviews, and on-site visits. The EEOC may issue a finding of "reasonable cause" to believe discrimination occurred, which often triggers further conciliation (settlement) efforts, or a "no cause" finding (dismissal of the charge). In either case, the EEOC will issue a Notice of Right to Sue, authorizing you to file a lawsuit in federal district court. You have only 90 days from receipt of the right-to-sue letter to file suit — another critical deadline that must not be missed.

Critical Deadlines Summary:

  • EEOC charge filing: 180 or 300 days from termination (depending on state)
  • Federal lawsuit after EEOC right-to-sue: 90 days from receipt of letter
  • ADEA lawsuit (if EEOC charge filed): 90 days from right-to-sue letter
  • State law claims: Varies by state — typically 1-3 years from termination
  • WARN Act claims: 3 years from the date WARN notice was required

However, having an experienced employment attorney assist you in drafting the charge is strongly advisable. The factual allegations in your charge form the legal foundation for any subsequent lawsuit — an incomplete or poorly drafted charge can limit your legal theories and harm your case later. Under Title VII and the ADA, you must wait until the EEOC issues a right-to-sue notice before filing a federal lawsuit. However, under the ADEA, you may file a lawsuit 60 days after filing the EEOC charge, even if the agency has not yet completed its investigation or issued a right-to-sue letter. Additionally, you may request a right-to-sue letter from the EEOC at any time after filing your charge (even before the investigation is complete), provided 180 days have passed since you filed. For state law claims, there is generally no need to exhaust EEOC remedies first.
A valid release of claims in a severance agreement is a powerful legal barrier to a wrongful termination lawsuit. However, not all releases are enforceable. For releases to be valid under the OWBPA (for employees over 40), they must: specifically mention ADEA claims, be written in plain language, give the employee at least 21 days to consider, provide a 7-day revocation period, and advise the employee to consult an attorney. Releases can also be voided if they were obtained through fraud, duress, or misrepresentation; if the employee was not given adequate consideration (the severance must be something beyond what the employee was already entitled to); or if the release does not comply with applicable state law requirements.
The single most time-sensitive action is to contact an employment attorney as quickly as possible — ideally within the first few days of termination. The EEOC charge filing deadlines begin running on the date of termination, and the investigation and legal strategy that your attorney develops depends on evidence that is most readily available immediately after the fact. Many employment attorneys offer free initial consultations. In the meantime, do not sign any separation agreements, preserve all documents you legitimately have access to, and write down everything you remember about the circumstances of your firing while your memory is fresh.

Section II: Mitigation of Damages — Your Legal Obligation to Seek New Work

Under the principle of mitigation, wrongfully terminated employees have a legal duty to make reasonable, good-faith efforts to minimize their damages by seeking new employment. Failure to mitigate can significantly reduce the amount of back pay and front pay a court will award. Here is what mitigation requires and does not require:

What Mitigation Requires

You must actively search for comparable employment. This typically means registering with the state employment agency, applying regularly to available positions that match your qualifications, attending job fairs, networking within your industry, and keeping detailed records of all your job search efforts. Courts will reduce your back pay by the amount you actually earned in substitute employment and, if you failed to make good-faith job search efforts, by the amount you could have earned with reasonable diligence.

What Mitigation Does NOT Require

You are not required to accept a position that is substantially inferior to your former job in terms of status, compensation, or working conditions — what the law calls "inferior employment." You are not required to move to a different city or state to find comparable work. You are not required to accept reinstatement to your former position if the working environment is so hostile that a reasonable person could not be expected to return. The employer bears the burden of proving that you failed to exercise reasonable diligence in your job search — you do not have to prove that you tried.
