What Happens During a Criminal Jury Trial?

What Happens During a Criminal Jury Trial?

Step-by-step guide to the phases of a criminal jury trial under the Sixth Amendment.

Jury Selection (Voir Dire)

The trial begins with selecting 12 jurors (plus alternates) from a pool of citizens. Attorneys use voir dire to question potential jurors, dismissing individuals for cause (bias) or using peremptory challenges to dismiss jurors without stating a reason.

Opening Statements and Witness Examination

Both sides present opening statements to summarize their case. The prosecution presents evidence first, calling witnesses who are subject to cross-examination by the defense. The defense is not required to call witnesses or present evidence.

Jury Deliberation, Verdict, and Sentencing

After closing arguments and receiving instructions from the judge, the jury deliberates in private. In criminal cases, the verdict must be unanimous to convict or acquit. If the jury cannot agree, the judge declares a mistrial (hung jury).

Key Legal Codes & Statutory Analysis

Sixth Amendment Rights to Counsel and Speedy Trial

The Sixth Amendment guarantees multiple critical protections for defendants facing criminal prosecution. These include the right to a speedy and public trial, the right to an impartial jury, the right to confront and cross-examine witnesses, and the right to the assistance of counsel. Under the Supreme Court's ruling in Gideon v. Wainwright, the state must provide a qualified defense attorney to any indigent defendant facing jail time. Defense attorneys utilize Sixth Amendment rights to challenge prosecution delays and ensure that the state provides all evidence through discovery before a trial begins.

Constitutional Reference: Federal Rules of Criminal Procedure Rules 23-31.

In criminal law, reference authorities like Federal Rules of Criminal Procedure Rules 23-31. establish procedural boundaries that law enforcement officers must follow. These rules safeguard defendants' constitutional rights during traffic stops, arrests, and custodial questioning. If a defense attorney proves that officers violated these rules, the court can suppress physical evidence or statements, which often weakens the prosecution's case and can lead to a reduction or dismissal of charges.

Case Study Analysis: Plea Bargaining and the Right to Speedy Trial

A defendant was indicted on felony charges and held in custody for six months due to an inability to post bail. The defense team filed a motion asserting a violation of the constitutional right to a speedy trial, pointing to multiple scheduling delays requested by the prosecution. In response, the state offered a negotiated plea bargain, reducing the charges to a misdemeanor with credit for time served.

After consulting with counsel, the defendant accepted the plea deal to secure immediate release rather than risking a trial. During the plea colloquy, the judge verified that the defendant was entering the plea voluntarily, understood the rights being waived, and admitted to a factual basis for the lesser charge. This resolution shows how pre-trial procedural pressure and constitutional rights shape the plea bargaining system, which resolves the vast majority of criminal cases in the United States.

Step-by-Step Action Plan: Handling the Arrest and Arraignment Process

Step 1: Assert Your Right to Remain Silent

Politely tell the booking officers: 'I am exercising my right to remain silent and I will not answer questions without a lawyer present.' Stop all conversations about the incident immediately.

Step 2: Request Legal Representation Immediately

Ask for a phone call to contact a criminal defense attorney or your family. Do not discuss the details of your case on the jail phones, as all calls are recorded and monitored by prosecutors.

Step 3: Cooperate with Booking and Fingerprinting

Provide basic biographical info (name, address, date of birth) and comply with fingerprinting and booking photographs. Resisting booking is a separate misdemeanor offense.

Step 4: Prepare for the Bail Hearing

Have your attorney compile evidence showing your ties to the community, family responsibilities, and stable employment to argue for Release on Recognizance (ROR) or a reasonable bail amount.

Step 5: Review the Charging Documents

Review the formal complaint or indictment during the arraignment. Ensure your attorney enters a plea of 'not guilty' to preserve all options and secure a discovery timeline.

Legal Glossary: Key Terms Explained

Arraignment
The first court appearance where a defendant is formally read their charges and asked to enter a plea (guilty, not guilty, or no contest).
Plea Bargain
A negotiated agreement where a defendant pleads guilty to a lesser charge in exchange for a lighter sentence or dismissed counts.
Speedy Trial
A Sixth Amendment guarantee protecting defendants from being held indefinitely by requiring the state to prosecute within a set timeframe.

Finding Local Criminal Defense Support

If you are facing criminal investigations or charges, obtaining legal representation immediately is critical. If you cannot afford a private criminal defense attorney, the Sixth Amendment guarantees the right to have a public defender appointed to your case at no cost if you meet financial eligibility guidelines. You can request a public defender during your initial appearance or arraignment. Additionally, local criminal courts maintain administrative self-help desks to explain scheduling, warrant checks, and expungement procedures, though they cannot provide direct legal representation or advice.
