Can I Reopen a Settled Personal Injury Case?

Understand the binding nature of settlement releases and the rare exceptions.

The Finality of the Release of Claims

When you accept a settlement, you sign a Release of All Claims form. This document releases the at-fault party and their insurer from all future liability. Once signed, the settlement is final, and you cannot seek more money even if your injuries worsen.

Rare Exceptions to the Rule

A settled case can only be reopened in rare circumstances: (1) if the settlement was obtained through fraud or misrepresentation; (2) if there was a mutual mistake of fact; or (3) if the release form was drafted in violation of state contract laws.

Conditional and Phased Settlements

In cases with ongoing medical treatment, your attorney can structure the settlement to remain open for a set period to cover future medical procedures, or delay settling until your medical recovery is complete and all costs are known.

Case Study Analysis: Navigating Civil Discovery and Insurance Bad Faith

An injured motorist filed a personal injury lawsuit after the liability insurer refused to pay a policy-limit demand, despite clear evidence of their driver's negligence and over $100,000 in medical bills. During the civil discovery phase, the plaintiff's attorney took depositions of the insurance adjusters and subpoenaed the internal claims file.

The internal records revealed that the insurer's automated claims software had scored the case as a high-value claim, but the supervisor instructed the adjuster to make a low-ball offer and delay communication to pressure the plaintiff into settling. Armed with this evidence of bad faith, the plaintiff's attorney amended the complaint to include an insurance bad faith cause of action. The insurer immediately settled the case for an amount far exceeding the initial policy limits, showing how aggressive civil discovery can expose bad faith and secure fair recovery.

Step-by-Step Action Plan: Navigating the Civil Discovery Phase

Step 1: Track the Statute of Limitations

Identify the strict deadline for filing a lawsuit in your state. Ensure your attorney drafts and files the formal complaint well before this date to preserve your legal rights.

Step 2: Answer Written Discovery Requests

Work with your attorney to answer written interrogatories and compile documents for requests for production, providing medical bills, tax returns, and accident logs under oath.

Step 3: Prepare for Oral Depositions

Attend pre-deposition prep sessions with your attorney. Practice answering questions honestly, briefly, and clearly, and learn how to handle trick questions from the defense lawyer.

Step 4: Attend Mandatory Court Mediation

Participate in structured mediation conferences led by a neutral mediator. Present your medical charts and financial logs to convince the insurer to make a reasonable settlement offer.

Step 5: Resolve Liens and Distribute Funds

Once settled, have your attorney negotiate and pay off health insurance and hospital liens from the trust account, and receive your final net settlement check.

Legal Glossary: Key Terms Explained

Preponderance of the Evidence
The civil burden of proof requiring that a claim is more likely true than not, representing a certainty level of over 50%.
Civil Discovery
The pre-trial phase of a lawsuit where both sides are legally required to exchange documents, answer written questions, and take depositions.
Implied Covenant of Good Faith
The legal duty of insurance companies to handle claims fairly, evaluate damages honestly, and communicate without unreasonable delays.

Finding Local Personal Injury Assistance

If you are seeking financial recovery after a severe injury, consulting a local personal injury attorney can clarify your rights. Most civil injury lawyers operate on a contingency fee basis, meaning they evaluate and handle cases with no upfront costs, taking a percentage of the final recovery. You can verify attorney credentials and disciplinary histories through your state's bar association portal. For immediate support, consult local legal clinics or check hospital social work departments, which help injured patients apply for temporary disability benefits, MedPay coverage, or regional medical care programs.
