Can a Prenuptial Agreement Be Challenged?

Prenuptial agreements are legally binding contracts, but they can be challenged and invalidated in court under specific legal grounds.

Understanding the Burden of Proof and UPAA Standards

A prenuptial (or premarital) agreement is a contract signed before marriage that determines how assets, debts, and spousal support will be handled in the event of a divorce. While courts generally presume prenups are valid, the spouse seeking to invalidate the agreement can file a challenge during the divorce proceeding. The burden of proof rests entirely on the challenging spouse to show that the agreement is unenforceable.

Most states have adopted the Uniform Premarital Agreement Act (UPAA), which establishes clear standards for enforcing prenups. Under the UPAA, an agreement is unenforceable if: (1) it was not signed voluntarily; (2) it was unconscionable when signed; (3) there was a lack of fair and reasonable disclosure of assets and debts; or (4) the challenging spouse did not waive their right to disclosure in writing.

Common Grounds for Challenging a Prenup

  • Duress or Coercion: Proving that you were forced or pressured into signing the agreement (such as being presented with the document the night before the wedding with threats to call off the ceremony).
  • Hidden Assets or Liabilities: If one spouse failed to make a full and honest financial disclosure, and the other spouse signed without knowing the true extent of the assets, the court may throw out the prenup.
  • Lack of Independent Legal Counsel: While not strictly required in all states, courts heavily scrutinize prenups where one spouse did not have their own attorney. Some states, like California, require independent counsel if spousal support rights are altered.

Key Legal Codes & Statutory Analysis

The Uniform Premarital Agreement Act (UPAA) Standards of Enforceability

The UPAA, adopted in 28 states and the District of Columbia, outlines strict rules for drafting and enforcing prenuptial agreements. Under the UPAA, an agreement is presumed valid but can be invalidated if the challenging spouse proves: (1) they did not sign the contract voluntarily; (2) the agreement was unconscionable when signed; or (3) they were not provided a fair and reasonable disclosure of the other spouse's assets and liabilities. The act places a heavy burden of proof on the challenging spouse. To ensure enforceability, estate planning attorneys recommend that both parties retain independent legal counsel and execute the agreement at least thirty days before the wedding date.

Case Study Analysis: Challenging Prenuptial Agreements in Court

During a high-conflict divorce action, the wife petitioned to invalidate a prenuptial agreement signed ten years prior. She asserted that the contract was signed under duress, as it was presented to her at the wedding rehearsal dinner, and that the husband had failed to provide a full and fair disclosure of his commercial real estate holdings, violating the Uniform Premarital Agreement Act (UPAA).

The family court held an evidentiary hearing to analyze the circumstances of the agreement's execution. The judge noted that the wife did not have independent legal counsel and was given less than 24 hours to review the document. Furthermore, financial records revealed the husband had undervalued his assets by over 40%. The court held that the agreement was unconscionable and executed under duress, invalidating the entire prenuptial agreement and opening the estate to standard equitable distribution rules, emphasizing the strict requirements for marital contract validity.

Step-by-Step Action Plan: Drafting a Valid Prenuptial Agreement

Step 1: Initiate Negotiations Early

Begin discussing the prenuptial agreement at least three to six months before the scheduled wedding date. Avoid presenting the document close to the ceremony, as it creates grounds for duress claims.

Step 2: Provide Full Financial Disclosures

Attach a complete list of all assets, debts, real estate holdings, business interests, and sources of income to the agreement. Hiding or understating assets will make the prenup invalid in court.

Step 3: Retain Independent Legal Counsel

Both partners must hire separate, independent family law attorneys to review and negotiate the terms. Joint representation or using a single lawyer will lead to the agreement being thrown out.

Step 4: Exclude Prohibited Provisions

Ensure the agreement does not contain any clauses regarding child custody, visitation, or child support, as these are determined solely by the judge using the best-interests standard at the time of divorce.

Step 5: Formally Execute the Agreement

Sign the final document in the presence of a notary public and witnesses. Retain physical copies and digital records of the signed agreement and the disclosures in secure locations.

Legal Glossary: Key Terms Explained

Uniform Premarital Agreement Act (UPAA)
A model statute adopted by many states that outlines the rules for executing valid and enforceable prenuptial agreements.
Unconscionability
A contract that is so extremely one-sided and unfair to one party that the court will refuse to enforce it on grounds of public policy.
Duress
Unlawful pressure or coercion exerted on a person to force them to sign a contract against their will, making the agreement invalid.
