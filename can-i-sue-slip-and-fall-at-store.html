Can I Sue for a Slip and Fall Accident at a Store?

Learn about premises liability laws, your rights after a slip and fall, and what you must prove to win.

Store Owner Duty of Care

Retail stores and businesses owe a legal duty to keep their premises reasonably safe for customers. This includes performing regular sweeps, placing warning signs near wet floors, and addressing hazards promptly.

Proving Notice of the Hazard

To win a slip and fall case, you must show the store had notice of the hazard. This means showing they either created the hazard, knew about it (actual notice), or the hazard existed long enough that they should have discovered it (constructive notice).

Documenting the Incident and Seeking Medical Care

Immediately after a fall, report the incident to the manager and ensure they write an incident report. Get a copy. Take pictures of the hazard and seek immediate medical evaluation to document your physical injuries.

Key Legal Codes & Statutory Analysis

The Application of the Restatement of Torts in Negligence Claims

The Restatement (Second) of Torts, published by the American Law Institute, is a highly authoritative legal treatise that compiles and organizes the common law rules of negligence and tort liability. Although not a statute, civil judges rely heavily on the Restatement to resolve complex legal disputes, such as defining the duty of care, determining proximate causation, and evaluating product liability designs. Key sections outline the 'eggshell plaintiff' rule, joint and several liability, and the standards for calculating pain and suffering, establishing a uniform framework for personal injury claims across the country.

Sixth Amendment Rights to Counsel and Speedy Trial

The Sixth Amendment guarantees multiple critical protections for defendants facing criminal prosecution. These include the right to a speedy and public trial, the right to an impartial jury, the right to confront and cross-examine witnesses, and the right to the assistance of counsel. Under the Supreme Court's ruling in Gideon v. Wainwright, the state must provide a qualified defense attorney to any indigent defendant facing jail time. Defense attorneys utilize Sixth Amendment rights to challenge prosecution delays and ensure that the state provides all evidence through discovery before a trial begins.

Case Study Analysis: Notice of Hazards in Premises Liability Claims

A customer sustained a severe hip fracture after slipping on a liquid spill in a department store aisle. The store's management denied liability, claiming the spill was recent and that they had no notice of the hazard. The plaintiff's attorney filed a civil lawsuit and subpoenaed the store's internal surveillance footage and employee sweep logs.

The surveillance video showed that the liquid spill was caused by another customer nearly two hours before the accident, and that three store employees had walked past the spill without cleaning it or placing warning signs. The employee sweep logs, which claimed the aisle was checked every thirty minutes, were proven to be falsified. The court ruled that the store had constructive notice of the hazard and had breached its duty of care, awarding full compensatory damages to the plaintiff, highlighting the evidentiary standards required for premises liability success.

Step-by-Step Action Plan: What to Do After a Slip and Fall

Step 1: Report the Incident and Get a Copy

Notify the store manager or property owner immediately. Request that they complete a formal incident report, and obtain a physical or digital copy of the report before leaving the property.

Step 2: Photograph the Hazard and Surroundings

Take close-up photos of the hazard (e.g. wet floor, ice patch, loose rug, broken step) and wider photos showing the lack of warning signs or poor lighting conditions in the area.

Step 3: Collect Witness Contact Info

Identify other customers or bystanders who saw you fall or witnessed the hazardous condition. Collect their names, phone numbers, and email addresses; their independent testimony is critical.

Step 4: Preserve Your Footwear and Clothing

Save the shoes and clothes you were wearing during the fall in a secure bag. Do not wear them again, as their condition (tread, lack of mud, etc.) can be analyzed to refute claims of comparative fault.

Step 5: File a Premises Liability Claim

Consult a personal injury lawyer to file a claim with the property owner's commercial liability insurance carrier, demanding the preservation of all surveillance videos and sweep logs.

Legal Glossary: Key Terms Explained

Premises Liability
The legal framework holding property owners and managers responsible for injuries caused by dangerous, neglected conditions on their land.
Constructive Notice
The legal doctrine holding that an owner should have known about a hazard because it existed long enough for a reasonable owner to discover it.
Slip and Fall
A civil personal injury claim arising when an individual is injured due to a slick surface, trip hazard, or fall on another's property.
